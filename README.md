# 🌍 CarbonPlate — Food Carbon Footprint Comparator

A web app that calculates the carbon footprint of your weekly meal plan, breaks it down by meal, ingredient, and production method, and compares it side-by-side with a plant-based equivalent.

**Data is accurate enough to cite in advocacy material**, using [Clark et al. (2022)](https://ourworldindata.org/environmental-impacts-of-food) as the primary dataset — 211 food items with GHG, land use, and water data from Our World in Data, underpinned by [Poore & Nemecek (2018)](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aao0216).

---

## What It Does

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Weekly meal planner** | 7-day × 4-meal-type grid; 30+ pre-built meal templates |
| **Portion size controls** | ±0.5 serving stepper on every meal card; emissions update live |
| **Custom meal builder** | Build any meal from 211 real ingredients with gram-level weights |
| **Carbon breakdown** | Per ingredient, per meal, per day, per category |
| **Plant-based comparison** | Automatic equivalent substitution (beef burger → black bean burger, etc.) |
| **Uncertainty ranges** | ±30% of mean (reflecting production system variation) |
| **Export** | PNG image, print/PDF, copy-to-clipboard stats + citation |
| **Methodology page** | Full emission factors table, calculation steps, limitations, citation guide |

## Product Thinking

Most carbon tools treat "food" as a single category. The reality is a beef steak has ~120× the emissions of lentils per kg. This tool makes that variance visceral and personal — you see *your* meals, broken down *by ingredient*, compared to *a specific alternative*. The plant-based comparison is not abstract ("eat less meat") but concrete ("your Tuesday spaghetti bolognese: 9.3 kg CO₂eq → lentil bolognese: 1.1 kg CO₂eq, saving 88%").

Users can also adjust portion sizes with the ±0.5 stepper on each meal, or build entirely custom meals from the full 211-ingredient dataset — so the tool reflects real eating patterns rather than fixed templates.

The shareable output and transparent methodology make it useful beyond personal reflection — as advocacy material or in research supporting dietary policy.

## Data Sources & Methodology

### Primary Dataset

> **Clark, M.A. et al. (2022).** *Global food system emissions could preclude achieving the 1.5° and 2°C climate change targets.* Science, 370(6517), 705–708. Via [Our World in Data](https://ourworldindata.org/environmental-impacts-of-food).

- **211 food items** with emission factors (kg CO₂eq/kg food)
- Additional metrics: land use (m²/kg), water use (L/kg), GHG per 1,000 kcal, GHG per 100g protein
- Source CSV: `src/data/clark2022.csv` — downloaded directly from the [OWID GitHub repository](https://github.com/owid/owid-datasets)
- `src/data/emissions.js` is **generated by parsing this CSV** — no hardcoded values

### Supporting Reference

> **Poore, J. & Nemecek, T. (2018).** *Reducing food's environmental impacts through producers and consumers.* Science, 360(6392), 987–992. [doi:10.1126/science.aao0216](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aao0216)

- 570 lifecycle assessment studies reviewed
- >38,000 farms, 119 countries, 40 food products
- Full supply-chain scope: land use change → farming → processing → transport → packaging

### Calculation Method

```
1. Ingredient weight:  grams per serving (from meal template or custom builder)
2. Adjust for servings: kg_food = grams × servings ÷ 1000
3. Apply factor:        kg_CO₂eq = kg_food × emission_factor (kg CO₂eq/kg food)
4. Uncertainty:         range = [kg_CO₂eq × 0.7, kg_CO₂eq × 1.3]  (±30%)
5. Aggregate:           weekly = Σ(all meals); annual = weekly × 52
6. Comparison:          saving = current_meal − plant_based_equivalent
```

### What's Included in Scope

✅ Land use change (deforestation for pasture/feed crops)
✅ On-farm operations (enteric fermentation, manure, fertilizer)
✅ Animal feed production
✅ Food processing and manufacturing
✅ Transport and packaging (to retail)

❌ Cooking energy (adds ~5–15%)
❌ Food waste emissions
❌ Consumer transport to shops

### Limitations

- **Global averages**: Figures are global means. Local, organic, or regenerative production varies substantially.
- **±30% uncertainty**: Clark et al. (2022) does not publish percentile ranges per item; ±30% reflects typical production system variance.
- **Per-kg, not per-calorie**: High-water vegetables appear worse on a per-calorie basis than in dietary terms.
- **Air freight not separated**: Out-of-season air-freighted produce can be 30–50× higher than the average shown.
- **2022 data**: Reflects the latest publicly available systematic dataset from Our World in Data.

## Design Decisions

**Why Clark et al. (2022) via Our World in Data?** The CSV is publicly available on the OWID GitHub repo, allowing the emission factors to be parsed directly from source rather than hardcoded — making the data provenance fully transparent and auditable.

**Why no backend?** All calculations are deterministic from static, versioned data. A backend adds complexity with no benefit.

**Why per-kg not per-calorie?** Clark et al. (2022) reports per-kg figures — this stays close to the citable source. Per-calorie would require nutritional data and make vegetables appear worse than they are in dietary terms.

**Why mean values?** Ranges (±30%) are shown in the uncertainty band but headlines use means — consistent with how the data is typically cited in subsequent research.

**Why ±30% uncertainty?** Clark et al. (2022) does not publish item-level percentile ranges. ±30% reflects the typical spread seen in Poore & Nemecek (2018) across production systems for most food categories.

**Why these plant-based equivalents?** Each was chosen to be a realistic, commonly available, nutritionally comparable substitute — not an exotic replacement. The goal is to show what a practical diet shift looks like.
